Ancient History

Illyrian Tribes in the Balkans

Map showing the distribution of major Illyrian tribes around 300 BCE

Pelasgians: The Earliest Roots

The Pelasgians are a bit of a mystery—ancient Greek sources like Homer and Herodotus describe them as a pre-Indo-European people in the Balkans and Aegean, possibly predating 2000 BCE. Some Albanian scholars and nationalists argue they were proto-Albanians, citing their presence in regions like Epirus and Thessaly, where Illyrians later emerged.

Evidence is thin—mostly myths and Greek references—but archaeological finds, like Bronze Age tools in southern Albania (e.g., Maliq), suggest early inhabitants. They likely lived in simple, tribal societies, farming and trading across the Balkans. No clear kings or polities are recorded, and their language is unknown, but some link their legacy to Albanian linguistic continuity, though this is speculative.

Illyrians: The Tribal Ancestors

By the Iron Age (circa 1000 BCE), the Illyrians, Indo-European tribes, dominated the western Balkans, including modern Albania. Albanians often see them as direct forebears due to geographic overlap, cultural continuity, and linguistic hints (e.g., shared place names like "Dardania").

The Illyrians were organized into tribes like the Taulantii, Ardiaei, Enchelei, Albanoi (near modern Tirana), and others, with key centers in Shkodra, Lissus, and Apollonia.

Gjergj Kastrioti Skanderbeg (1405–1468)

The national hero of Albania. Born to the Kastrioti family, he was raised as an Ottoman hostage but defected in 1443 during the Battle of Niš, seizing Kruja. He formed the League of Lezhë (1444), uniting Albanian nobles against the Ottomans.

Skanderbeg's guerrilla tactics and victories, like the Battle of Torvioll (1444) and sieges of Kruja (1450, 1466–67), held off Ottoman armies under Murad II and Mehmed II. His alliances with Venice and Naples made him a European legend.

After his death in 1468, resistance crumbled, and Albania fell fully to the Ottomans by 1479. Many Albanians fled to Italy, forming Arbëreshë communities that preserve Albanian traditions to this day.

Skanderbeg's Legacy

Skanderbeg's resistance against the Ottoman Empire became a symbol of Albanian resilience and national identity. His helmet with a goat's head emblem is now a national symbol, and his story inspired generations of Albanian patriots.

Today, Skanderbeg is celebrated not only in Albania but also in Kosovo, North Macedonia, and among Albanian diaspora communities worldwide. His statue stands prominently in Skanderbeg Square in Tirana, the main square of Albania's capital.

Modern History

Ottoman Rule (1479–1912)

Ottoman Albania saw widespread Islamization, especially among elites via the timar system, though Christians (Catholic and Orthodox) persisted in mountains. Albanians like Ali Pasha of Tepelenë (1740–1822) rose as semi-independent rulers.

The 19th-century Albanian Renaissance (Rilindja) sparked national consciousness through figures like Naum Veqilharxhi and the Frashëri brothers, promoting Albanian language and culture.

Independence and World Wars

Albania declared independence on November 28, 1912, in Vlorë, led by Ismail Qemali, amid the Ottoman Empire's collapse during the First Balkan War. The London Conference (1913) recognized Albania but ceded Kosovo to Serbia, fueling tensions.

World War I (1914–1918) saw Albania occupied by multiple powers (Italy, Serbia, Austria-Hungary). Post-war, Albania stabilized under Ahmet Zogu, who became King Zog I in 1928 but leaned on Italian support.

World War II and Communist Era

World War II (1939–1945): Italy invaded in 1939, making Albania a protectorate under Victor Emmanuel III. Resistance grew under communist leader Enver Hoxha, alongside non-communist groups like Balli Kombëtar. By 1944, Hoxha's partisans liberated Albania, establishing a communist state.

Hoxha ruled from 1944 to 1985, isolating Albania with strict Stalinist policies, banning religion, and aligning briefly with Yugoslavia, the USSR, and China before breaking ties. His regime built thousands of bunkers, fearing invasion.

Post-Communist Transition

After his death, Ramiz Alia eased policies, but communism collapsed in 1991 amid protests. The 1990s were chaotic, with economic crises and the 1997 pyramid scheme collapse sparking unrest.

Since 1991, Albania transitioned to democracy, joining NATO in 2009 and becoming an EU candidate in 2014. It faces challenges like corruption and emigration but has stabilized politically. The Albanian diaspora, especially in Italy, Greece, and the U.S., remains influential.

Kosovo Liberation Army and Conflicts

Kosovo Liberation Army (UCK)

The Kosovo Liberation Army (Ushtria Çlirimtare e Kosovës, or UÇK) was an ethnic Albanian separatist militia that fought for the independence of Kosovo from the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia in the 1990s.

The UÇK emerged in the early 1990s, with its first public appearance in 1995-1996. Its campaign escalated significantly in 1997-1999, leading to the Kosovo War (1998-1999). The conflict ended with NATO intervention against Yugoslavia, resulting in the establishment of the United Nations Interim Administration Mission in Kosovo (UNMIK).

After the war, the UÇK was disbanded and transformed into the Kosovo Protection Corps, a civilian emergency response organization. Many former UÇK members entered politics, including Hashim Thaçi, who became Kosovo's first prime minister and later president.

Adem Jashari (1955–1998)

Adem Jashari was one of the founders of the Kosovo Liberation Army (KLA), a Kosovo Albanian separatist militia which fought for the secession of Kosovo from Yugoslavia during the 1990s.

Beginning in 1991, Jashari participated in attacks against the Serbian police before travelling to Albania to receive military training. Arrested in 1993, he was released and later returned to Kosovo, where he continued launching attacks against the Yugoslav establishment.

In March 1998, Serbian police launched an attack against Jashari's home in Prekaz. The battle that followed resulted in the deaths of 58 members of Jashari's family, including Jashari himself. He is regarded by many in Kosovo as the "father of the KLA" and a symbol of Kosovar independence.

Notable Figures

Gjergj Kastrioti (Skanderbeg)
National Hero (1405–1468)

Led Albanian resistance against the Ottoman Empire for 25 years, successfully defending Albania against numerous invasions. His military genius and leadership made him a symbol of Albanian unity and resistance.

Adem Jashari
KLA Commander (1955–1998)

One of the founders of the Kosovo Liberation Army, symbol of Kosovar resistance against Serbian forces. His sacrifice at Prekaz became a rallying cry for Kosovo's independence movement.

Ismail Qemali
Founding Father (1844–1919)

Led the Albanian Declaration of Independence in 1912 and served as the first Prime Minister of Albania. He was a key figure in the Albanian National Awakening and diplomatic efforts for international recognition.
