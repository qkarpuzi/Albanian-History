Ancient History

Pelasgians: The Earliest Roots

The Pelasgians are a bit of a mystery—ancient Greek sources like Homer and Herodotus describe them as a pre-Indo-European people in the Balkans and Aegean, possibly predating 2000 BCE :cite[1]:cite[4]. Some Albanian scholars and nationalists argue they were proto-Albanians, citing their presence in regions like Epirus and Thessaly, where Illyrians later emerged :cite[7].

Evidence is thin—mostly myths and Greek references—but archaeological finds, like Bronze Age tools in southern Albania (e.g., Maliq), suggest early inhabitants. They likely lived in simple, tribal societies, farming and trading across the Balkans. No clear kings or polities are recorded, and their language is unknown, but some link their legacy to Albanian linguistic continuity, though this is speculative :cite[1]:cite[4].

Illyrians: The Tribal Ancestors

By the Iron Age (circa 1000 BCE), the Illyrians, Indo-European tribes, dominated the western Balkans, including modern Albania :cite[2]:cite[5]. Albanians often see them as direct forebears due to geographic overlap, cultural continuity, and linguistic hints (e.g., shared place names like "Dardania").

The Illyrians were organized into tribes like the Taulantii, Ardiaei, Enchelei, Albanoi (near modern Tirana), and others, with key centers in Shkodra, Lissus, and Apollonia :cite[2].

Skanderbeg: Albania's National Hero

Gjergj Kastrioti Skanderbeg (1405–1468)

The national hero. Born to the Kastrioti family, he was raised as an Ottoman hostage but defected in 1443 during the Battle of Niš, seizing Kruja. He formed the League of Lezhë (1444), uniting Albanian nobles against the Ottomans :cite[6].

Skanderbeg's guerrilla tactics and victories, like the Battle of Torvioll (1444) and sieges of Kruja (1450, 1466–67), held off Ottoman armies under Murad II and Mehmed II. His alliances with Venice and Naples made him a European legend :cite[6].

After his death in 1468, resistance crumbled, and Albania fell fully to the Ottomans by 1479. Many Albanians fled to Italy, forming Arbëreshë communities.

"I have not brought you liberty, I found it here, among you."
- Skanderbeg

Modern History

Ottoman Rule (1479–1912)

Ottoman Albania saw widespread Islamization, especially among elites via the timar system, though Christians (Catholic and Orthodox) persisted in mountains. Albanians like Ali Pasha of Tepelenë (1740–1822) rose as semi-independent rulers.

The 19th-century Albanian Renaissance (Rilindja) sparked national consciousness through figures like Naum Veqilharxhi and the Frashëri brothers, promoting Albanian language and culture.

Independence and World Wars

Albania declared independence on November 28, 1912, in Vlorë, led by Ismail Qemali, amid the Ottoman Empire's collapse during the First Balkan War. The London Conference (1913) recognized Albania but ceded Kosovo to Serbia, fueling tensions.

World War I (1914–1918) saw Albania occupied by multiple powers (Italy, Serbia, Austria-Hungary). Post-war, Albania stabilized under Ahmet Zogu, who became King Zog I in 1928 but leaned on Italian support.

World War II and Communist Era

World War II (1939–1945): Italy invaded in 1939, making Albania a protectorate under Victor Emmanuel III. Resistance grew under communist leader Enver Hoxha, alongside non-communist groups like Balli Kombëtar. By 1944, Hoxha's partisans liberated Albania, establishing a communist state.

Hoxha ruled from 1944 to 1985, isolating Albania with strict Stalinist policies, banning religion, and aligning briefly with Yugoslavia, the USSR, and China before breaking ties. His regime built thousands of bunkers, fearing invasion.

Post-Communist Transition

After his death, Ramiz Alia eased policies, but communism collapsed in 1991 amid protests. The 1990s were chaotic, with economic crises and the 1997 pyramid scheme collapse sparking unrest.

Since 1991, Albania transitioned to democracy, joining NATO in 2009 and becoming an EU candidate in 2014. It faces challenges like corruption and emigration but has stabilized politically. The Albanian diaspora, especially in Italy, Greece, and the U.S., remains influential.

Kosovo Liberation Army (UCK) and 2001 Macedonia Conflict

Kosovo Liberation Army (UCK)

The Kosovo Liberation Army (Ushtria Çlirimtare e Kosovës, or UÇK) was an ethnic Albanian separatist militia that fought for the independence of Kosovo from the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia in the 1990s :cite[8].

The UÇK emerged in the early 1990s, with its first public appearance in 1995-1996. Its campaign escalated significantly in 1997-1999, leading to the Kosovo War (1998-1999). The conflict ended with NATO intervention against Yugoslavia, resulting in the establishment of the United Nations Interim Administration Mission in Kosovo (UNMIK).

After the war, the UÇK was disbanded and transformed into the Kosovo Protection Corps, a civilian emergency response organization. Many former UÇK members entered politics, including Hashim Thaçi, who became Kosovo's first prime minister and later president.

Adem Jashari (1955–1998)

Adem Jashari was one of the founders of the Kosovo Liberation Army (KLA), a Kosovo Albanian separatist militia which fought for the secession of Kosovo from Yugoslavia during the 1990s :cite[8].

Beginning in 1991, Jashari participated in attacks against the Serbian police before travelling to Albania to receive military training. Arrested in 1993, he was released at the behest of the Albanian Army and later returned to Kosovo, where he continued launching attacks against the Yugoslav establishment.

In July 1997, he was convicted of terrorism in absentia by a Yugoslav court. After several unsuccessful attempts to capture or kill him, Serbian police launched an attack against Jashari's home in Prekaz in March 1998. The battle that followed resulted in the deaths of 58 members of Jashari's family, including that of Jashari, his wife, brother and son.

"Seen as the 'father of the KLA', Jashari is considered a symbol of Kosovar independence by ethnic Albanians."
- Wikipedia

Jashari is regarded by many in Kosovo as being the "father of the KLA". Portraits of him carrying an automatic weapon often adorn the walls of homes inhabited by ethnic Albanians. Considered a symbol of independence by Kosovo Albanians, the anniversary of Jashari's death is annually commemorated in Kosovo.

Hashim Thaçi

Hashim Thaçi is a Kosovar Albanian politician who was the prime minister of Kosovo from 2008 to 2014 and the president of Kosovo from 2016 to 2020. He was a leader of the Kosovo Liberation Army (KLA) during the Kosovo War.

Thaçi was one of the participants in the Rambouillet peace talks in 1999 that sought to resolve the Kosovo crisis. After the war, he entered politics and became the leader of the Democratic Party of Kosovo (PDK).

In April 2016, Thaçi was elected president by the Parliament of Kosovo. His presidency was marked by efforts to normalize relations with Serbia and attempts to advance Kosovo's international recognition. In 2020, he was indicted by the Kosovo Specialist Chambers for war crimes and crimes against humanity during the Kosovo War, leading to his resignation.

"Thaçi was a central figure in the Kosovo Liberation Army and later in Kosovar politics."

Despite his controversial legacy and indictment, Thaçi remains a significant figure in modern Kosovar history and the struggle for independence.

2001 Macedonia Conflict

The 2001 Macedonia conflict was an armed conflict between the Macedonian government and ethnic Albanian insurgents, primarily from the National Liberation Army (NLA). The conflict lasted from January to November 2001, ending with the Ohrid Agreement.

The NLA's goals included greater rights and autonomy for the ethnic Albanian minority in Macedonia, which comprised about 25% of the population. The insurgents sought to address issues of discrimination and unequal treatment.

The conflict began in January 2001 when the NLA attacked police and army facilities in mainly Albanian-populated areas. The fighting intensified throughout the year, with the NLA taking control of territories in northern and western Macedonia.

Ohrid Agreement and Aftermath

The Ohrid Framework Agreement was signed on August 13, 2001, by the Macedonian government and Albanian representatives. The agreement:

  • Granted greater rights to the Albanian minority
  • Made Albanian an official language in areas with significant Albanian population
  • Increased Albanian representation in government and security forces
  • Provided for decentralization of government

After the agreement, the NLA officially disbanded in November 2001, with many members handing over their weapons to NATO forces. The conflict resulted in approximately 200 deaths and displaced tens of thousands of people. The Ohrid Agreement successfully ended the violence and has been largely successful in maintaining peace since.
